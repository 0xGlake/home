# Just-in-time vs just-in-case information

There are two ways information enters your life. One arrives because you went looking for it — a problem in front of you, a decision to make, a thing you are trying to build, and you reach out and pull exactly what you need into the work. The other arrives because a feed delivered it, a recommendation surfaced it, or you stored it away on the theory that it might be useful someday. Both feel like learning. Only one usually is.

Just-in-time information is pulled by action. A programmer opening documentation to write the next line. A founder interrogating models and operators because the company will not build itself. The defining feature is that the search is driven by an objective outside the information itself — you want to _do_ something, and knowledge is the shortest path there. This mode has an underrated property: doing generates the appetite for learning, not the other way around. Pure learning for its own sake goes flat after a while because nothing is pulling on it.

Just-in-case information is the larger category and the more dangerous one. Some of it is genuinely useful — an analyst absorbing low-level detail to make a big decision later, or an investor watching adjacent markets. But most of it is the candy version: the self-development reel, the explainer thread, the productivity breakdown, the 'interesting' article saved to a list that will never be read. It feels productive because it resembles productive activity, but it only counts if it eventually changes what you do. Otherwise it is bandwidth that could have been spent elsewhere.

The feeds that serve this category are adversarial. They are tuned by people whose job is to keep you in them. A variable reward schedule will beat your willpower every time, and the fact that the content is low-friction is itself a warning: if it is easy for you, it is easy for everyone, and a mind fed only the things everyone else is being fed becomes indistinguishable from theirs. Differentiation is downstream of what you take in.

Exploration is the honest exception. Not every input has to be aimed at an outcome. Wandering, art, fiction, long-form reading — these are productive precisely because they are not measured by immediate yield. A good book can rearrange how you see the world in a way no compiler reference ever will, and a career can turn on one of those rearrangements. The risk is the inverse: a book installs a map, and the wrong map confidently followed is worse than no map at all. The danger of reading is not that you will waste the afternoon; it is that you will absorb a model of the world that does not match the territory and start acting on it.

This is why memory is really information plus emotion. Neutral information does not stick because there is no evaluative hook for it to hang on. What lodges is what you _cared_ about at the moment of encounter, what connected to a premise you already held or a goal you were already chasing. The implication for an information diet is straightforward: consumption at low emotional engagement is consumption that mostly evaporates. You are not getting what you think you are getting.

The useful contrast is between lakes and rivers. Lakes compound. They are fed by hierarchical knowledge — things that reduce cleanly to evidence you can point at, and that support further structures above them. Rivers rush past. They carry takes, reactions, headlines, opinions floating above any base, and they leave little behind because there is nothing to attach them to. A healthy mind runs mostly on the lake, dips into rivers when there is a reason, and treats exploration as a separate budget.

A simple test for anything you are about to consume: does it connect to something you are doing, something you are building, or something you are trying to become? If not, is it feeding the lake or just passing through? The food you eat becomes your body. The information you consume becomes your mind. Audit accordingly.

Information is useful if it aligns your internal world view to the actual world, and the fastest way to test that alignment is to move within the world and see what breaks.
