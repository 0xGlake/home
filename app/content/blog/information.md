# Information: Looking at the road and staying in your lane

*This is part two of a series on attention. It stands alone, but it builds on [part one, about agency](/blog/agency), which I'd recommend reading first.*

In the first essay I argued that your attention has a steering wheel and your hands can be on it. This one is about where everybody drives.

## We are so lucky

I'm pretty sure the guy next to me at the gym is running my exact program. Not one like it, mine: same exercises, same order, same rep ranges, down to the three-minute rest timer, because we both got it from the same video, made by a guy who actually reads the studies, Jeff Nippard probably. More than half this gym is on creatine and considering peptides. Scan the floor and almost everyone is within a program or two of each other, all descended from the same few evidence-based channels.

And it's great. Twenty years ago you bought a DVD from a bodybuilder on undisclosed pharmaceuticals and did his routine, wrong, for a decade. Today a sixteen-year-old with a phone gets better programming on day one, for free, than professionals had in the nineties (imagine if Zyzz were born just five years later). We are so lucky. Attention is, minute by minute, the material your life is made of. Lately I keep noticing what everyone's is pointed at: the same things. Frequently the best things.

## Priced in

The gym is just where the pattern is easiest to spot. Someone publishes the best build, and everyone plays the best build. Gamers have known this for years: a patch drops, the min-maxers solve it, and by the weekend every ranked lobby is running the same meta. Looksmaxxing sat in obscurity for a decade as basement lore, then went mainstream in about a fortnight. The pipeline from obscure edge to common knowledge has never been shorter.

That speed quietly kills something: asymmetry. Information has the most edge while you have it and others don't. Anything legible enough to be ranked, packaged and recommended is already priced in by the time it reaches you. It landed in your feed because it landed in everyone's. **You're not early, you're exit liquidity.** Are you considering peptides because they serve some plan of yours, or because they're the current thing? Not that anyone is actually competing in the gym; unless you're a career bodybuilder there's no podium, and the best build going public is pure win. But where you do compete, in careers, companies, markets, being interchangeable gets expensive. There, the best available information isn't an advantage, it's the cost of entry.

## The convergence

Stack enough of those optimised choices and a person starts to assemble:

- Finance degree
- AI startup
- Climbing on the weekends
- 5g creatine
- Paycheck auto-invested into the S&P 500
- Mitski in the headphones
- Hinge date lined up on Saturday

Every individual choice is defensible. Nothing went wrong, and yet you'd swear you've met them before. You have: the performative male, just one of many archetypes.

**Everyone thinks they're esoteric because of the video essays they watch.** I'd love to be exempt here, but I counted how many items in that list are mine and I'm not telling. This is the actual fear under all the NPC memes. It's that you could do everything right, optimise every department of your life with the best public information, and wake up feeling interchangeable.

## The error is upstream

The tempting fix is to optimise less. That's wrong, optimising is what you should do: it buys back hours of your one life to spend on more things you actually value. Taking the best build off the shelf is just rational. The error is never in the means. It's upstream, in where the goals originated. The feed doesn't only deliver better methods; left unattended, it installs the ends too. Watch enough of it and you don't just learn the fastest route to the physique, the startup, the taste in music. You learn that those are the things to want, and from the inside it never feels like installation. **It feels like taste.**

This isn't an internet invention either; the internet just raised the velocity. The kid who picks finance because his parents wanted it, spends a decade competing against everyone else whose parents wanted it too, then surfaces mid-crisis at a muay thai camp in Thailand, ran the same bug on an older delivery system. And contrarianism is not the patch. Doing the opposite of the crowd still lets the crowd set your coordinates. In the first essay I called this drift: your hands slipping off the wheel of your attention. This is drift one level deeper. The capture was never really your hours. It's your wants, and people running towards wants that were never theirs do not end up happy.

## Direction, premises, action

Where does the edge live, then? Start by noticing what information is: just information. There is no noble category of content and no shameful one, no hierarchy where documentaries beat TikTok. The worth of any piece of it is set by the state of the person consuming, not the type of the thing consumed. Live earnings data is alpha mid-trade and trivia otherwise. A film can redirect a life or fill a Tuesday. The question is never what the content is. It's whether anything in you is currently aiming.

Next, premises. Everyone thinks intelligence is something you can buy now, and fair enough, the models are astonishing. But AI is a genius implementer of whatever premise you hand it. Build from a false one and the wheels turn beautifully with no traction. Premises also decide what information means: the same earnings call is alpha to an investor and noise to a novice, and the novice's fallback, asking a model or a professional he can't verify, is borrowed judgment. Verification is what fundamentals buy you. Your premises come from your information diet, the same channel your goals got installed through. Treat it like an attack surface.

Last, the part that compounds: act. The road most travelled is where all the competition is, and feeds only show roads. Doing generates private information, the kind that can't be scrolled to: what the customer actually said, what broke at 2am, whether the thing everyone recommends is even good. It's the only real test of your premises against the world. It's also where all remaining differentiation lives. Bill Gates and I have roughly the same access to food, media and locations, thanks to capitalism. What I'll never have is his position, and everything unique about it was **created, not consumed**. He wasn't chasing unique. He was chasing production and became memorable and unique as a byproduct.

## Trust your own mind

So: get clear on what you actually want, which is rare and makes clarity alone nearly uncontested alpha. Consume in service of it. Act, because acting teaches what no feed can. And through all of it, trust your own mind.
